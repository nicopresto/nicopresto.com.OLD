Nicholas David Preston, Ph.D.

[Photo credit - J.Skewes]

I am a postdoctoral scientist at the UW-Madison Center for Sustainability and the Global Environment (SAGE). My research focuses on the intersection of the environment, computing, and health.


Current Research Summary

In 2009, I joined SAGE as the project director for Healthscapes—a suite of web tools for finding, analyzing, and sharing global environmental health information. Our goal is to initiate a community-driven open source project to facilitate research on the environmental determinants of global health. Our research is currently being applied to infectious disease outbreaks and health monitoring during natural disasters. Healthsacpes is in an early release for developers; we aim to extend a stable release to the research community by summer 2011. For more information please visit healthscapes.org

{{=T("Undergraduate Studies (1997-2002)")}}

In 2002, I completed my B.Sc. in Biological Sciences at the University of Guelph (Ontario, Canada). My undergraduate minor was in Mathematical Sciences, a combination of statistics, mathematics, and computer science.

During my undergraduate studies I worked for Dr. Tim Johnson a limnologist at the OMNR Wheatley Fisheries Station. I was primarily charged with building a database of physical-chemical and zooplankton data for Lake Erie. The challenges synchronizing databases among the U.S. and Canadian agencies working in Lake Erie, fueled my life-long interest in database driven web design for science.

{{=T("Work in Guatemala (2002-2004)")}}

In 2002, I was sent to Guatemala, as an intern, to provide technical assistance to the Water Resource Manaagemnt Masters Program at the University of San Carlos. Following my internship, I established an NGO with Marvin Romero (of Solola, Guatemala), to build low-cost waste-treatment plants in rural Mayan villages. Our treatment systems were designed to mimic the natural processes of wetlands and rivers. Consequently, in the interest of furthering my understanding of these systems, I elected to pursue graduate studies in aquatic science (limnology).

{{=T("Graduate Studies (2004-2009)")}}

In 2009, I completed my Ph.D. at the Center for Limnology (CFL), UW-Madison (Wisconsin, USA). My research focused on external influences on lakes. Specifically, the influence of forests and the atmosphere on carbon cycling in lakes, and the influence of climate variability on ice cover and zooplankton. During my tenure as a graduate student, I also completed a certificate in Global Health. My motivation was to connect my understanding of pristine lakes to global health issues realted to water quality.

During my graduate studies, I developed an interest in the evolution of research toward data-intensive science. For instance, at the start of my graduate career we visited the lakes every few hours to collect measurements. By the end of my Ph.D., we had sensors and cameras throughout the lakes. Given my background in information management and scientific computing, I became fascinated by the potential of big data and have made this a central tenet of my current research. Similarly, in the wake of concerns over transparency in science, I began studying open source software development with an eye towards open science
